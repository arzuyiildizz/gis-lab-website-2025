Air Pollution Monitoring in Austria

WORKFLOW

Study Area:

How were the maps and charts on air pollution and population exposure created? Here, you’ll find the process step by step, beginning with data downloads, followed by analysis in QGIS, and ending with a fully interactive WebGIS platform.

Data Sources

The analysis relies on open-source geospatial datasets obtained from internationally recognized platforms. All data were downloaded, pre-processed, and harmonized in QGIS to support spatial analysis and mapping.

Air Quality Data NO₂, PM₂.₅, and PM₁₀:
  • Source: Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service
(CAMS):
  • Years: 2013–2022
  • Format: GeoTIFF (.tif)
  • Note: Monthly, annual average, and anomaly (AAD) layers were used for each pollutant.
Land Cover Data:
  • Source: ESA Climate Change Initiative (CCI) Land Cover Dataset
  • Year: 2022
  • Format: NetCDF (.nc), converted to GeoTIFF (.tif)
  • Note: The data was reclassified to identify agriculture, forest, grassland, wetland, settlement and other relevant land cover types.
Population Data:
  • Source: WorldPop Project
  • Year: 2020
  • Format: GeoTIFF (.tif)
  • Note: The raster layer represents estimated population counts at 100-meter resolution.
Administrative Boundaries:
  • Source: Global Administrative Unit Layers (GAUL) Dataset
  • Country: Austria
  • Levels Used
  • Level 0 – National Boundary (for masking and display purposes)
  • Level 2 – District Boundaries (used for zonal statistics and exposure aggregation)
  • Format: Shapefile (.shp)
Additional Information:
  • Coordinate Reference System (CRS): EPSG:4326 – WGS 84
  • Country: Austria
  • Units: degrees
  • Method: Geographic Coordinate System
  • Celestial Body: Earth
  • Accuracy: Based on World Geodetic System 1984 (EPSG:4326), suitable for global-scale spatial analysis with standard positional accuracy.

Step 1 – Monthly Aggregated Pollutant Maps (December 2022)

  • This first step involves the creation of monthly average pollutant concentration maps for NO₂, PM₂.₅, and PM₁₀ for December 2022. These datasets were obtained from the Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service (CAMS) as NetCDF files and processed in QGIS.
  • To produce the final raster maps, first, the CAMS NetCDF datasets were imported into QGIS as mesh layers. Using the Mesh Calculator, we computed the monthly average for December 2022. The resulting mesh was rasterized to GeoTIFF format and clipped to the boundaries of Austria using the GAUL Level 0 shapefile. Final outputs were styled using a sequential color gradient to indicate variation in concentration levels across regions.
  • Below are the final outputs for each pollutant:
NO₂ Concentration – December 2022
  • The map indicates higher concentrations of nitrogen dioxide in the eastern and southeastern regions of Austria, likely reflecting higher urban and traffic density.
PM₂.₅ Concentration – December 2022
  • This map shows elevated PM₂.₅ concentrations particularly in the northern and eastern regions of Austria, indicating potential air quality risks due to fine particulate matter exposure.
PM₁₀ Concentration – December 2022
  • PM₁₀ distribution shows significant levels across central and eastern Austria, suggesting a correlation with industrial and urban zones.

Step 2 – Yearly Aggregated Pollutant Maps (2022)

  • In this step, yearly average concentration maps for NO₂, PM₂.₅, and PM₁₀ were generated for the year 2022. These maps provide a more stable and representative overview of pollutant exposure over time, helping to smooth out short-term fluctuations observed in monthly data.
  • The process began by aggregating monthly pollutant raster datasets from January to December 2022. Using the r.series tool in QGIS, a mean operation was applied to combine all twelve monthly GeoTIFFs into one annual average raster for each pollutant. Each output was then clipped to Austria’s national boundary and styled with a continuous color ramp to highlight areas of high and low concentration.
  • Below are the final yearly maps for 2022 for each pollutant:
NO₂ – Yearly Average Concentration (2022)
  • This map shows elevated NO₂ levels primarily in the eastern part of Austria, corresponding with high-density urban and transport zones.
PM₂.₅ – Yearly Average Concentration (2022)
  • Annual PM₂.₅ concentrations are higher in southeastern Austria. These fine particulates pose a significant health risk, especially in regions with dense population and limited air circulation.
PM₁₀ – Yearly Average Concentration (2022)
  • Annual PM₁₀ levels are notably higher in industrial and urban zones, especially across central and eastern Austria.

Step 3 - Yearly Concentration Classification Maps (2020)

  • This step involves the classification of air pollutant concentrations for NO₂, PM₂.₅, and PM₁₀ for the year 2020. The annual average pollutant maps were reclassified into concentration classes based on the health guidelines defined by the European Union.
  • The reclassification was carried out in QGIS using the “Reclassify by Table” tool, applying defined threshold values for each pollutant. These thresholds were then used to generate discrete concentration maps that facilitate visual comparison of pollution levels across Austria.

  • The NO₂ map displays two concentration classes: Class 1 (≤ 40 µg/m³), covers the majority of the country, shown in red. Class 2 (> 40 µg/m³), localized regions with higher pollution, mostly in central and northeastern Austria, shown in blue. These regions exceed the EU annual limit value for NO₂ (40 µg/m³), primarily aligning with urbanized and traffic-dense zones.
PM₂.₅ – Concentration Classes (2020)
  • The PM₂.₅ map presents three classes: Class 1 (≤ 10 µg/m³), shown in red, representing lower exposure areas. Class 2 (10–25 µg/m³), dominant across Austria, shown in yellow. Class 3 (> 25 µg/m³), concentrated in eastern and southeastern districts, shown in blue. The presence of Class 3 regions reveals high PM₂.₅ exposure, exceeding WHO recommended limits and posing a significant public health risk.
PM₁₀ – Concentration Classes (2020)
  • The PM₁₀ map shows two concentration classes: Class 1 (≤ 40 µg/m³), widespread across the country, in red. Class 2 (> 40 µg/m³), sparse, visible in limited eastern zones, in blue. PM₁₀ levels remain mostly within acceptable ranges across Austria, with only a few exceedance zones.

Step 4 – Annual Average Difference Maps (2022)

  • This step aimed to assess how pollutant concentrations in 2022 differed from the recent historical average. To do this, the annual mean for each pollutant in 2022 was compared to the average of the five-year period between 2017 and 2021. The result is an anomaly map, highlighting areas where air quality either improved or worsened.

  • In QGIS, this was performed using the Raster Calculator by subtracting the 5-year average raster (2017–2021) from the 2022 raster for each pollutant. The resulting difference maps were styled using a diverging color scale: Blue tones indicate improvements (lower pollution in 2022 than the historical average), Red tones highlight deteriorations (higher pollution in 2022).

  • Below are the resulting maps and brief interpretations:

NO₂ – Annual Average Difference (2022 vs. 2017–2021)
  • Overall, NO₂ concentrations in Austria showed a slight improvement in 2022. Many areas, especially in the central and northeastern regions, experienced a decrease in nitrogen dioxide levels (blue tones), while some parts in the west and southeast showed minor increases (light red tones).
PM₂.₅ – Annual Average Difference (2022 vs. 2017–2021)
  • The PM₂.₅ anomaly map presents a clear north-south contrast. The northern and eastern zones exhibited declining PM₂.₅ levels (in blue), while southern and western Austria experienced higher-than-average concentrations in 2022 (in red), indicating possible localized pollution events or seasonal emissions.
PM₁₀ – Annual Average Difference (2022 vs. 2017–2021)
  • The pattern for PM₁₀ shows modest increases across central and southern Austria, with most of the country represented in light red. Only a few regions in the north show minor improvements. This suggests PM₁₀ levels remained relatively stable but with localized increases worth further attention.

Step 5 – Land Cover Classification and Reclassification

  • In this step, ESA CCI Land Cover data for the year 2022 was reclassified into simplified land use categories based on the IPCC classification schema. The purpose of this process was to better align land cover types with categories that are more meaningful for environmental and exposure analysis (e.g., Settlement, Forest, Agriculture).

  • First, the raw NetCDF dataset from the Copernicus Climate Data Store was imported into QGIS. The variable lccs_class was selected and visualized using unique values. The raster was clipped to Austria’s national boundary (GAUL Level 0). A reclassification table was applied using the “Reclassify by Table” tool in QGIS to convert raw class codes into six general land use categories (Settlement, Agriculture, Forest, Grassland, Wetland, Other).

Step 6 – Zonal Statistics and Time-Series Trend Analysis (2013–2022)

  • In this step, spatial statistical analyses were conducted to evaluate long-term trends of air pollutant concentrations (NO₂, PM₂.₅, PM₁₀) specifically over urban settlement areas. The goal was to assess how pollution levels have changed in built-up zones over a 10-year period, offering insights into persistent exposure risks in urban environments.

  • The reclassified land cover map (Step 5) was used to isolate the settlement class. The “Settlement” raster layer was polygonized to a vector and dissolved into a single urban area mask.

  • Below are the resulting maps and brief interpretations:

Extracted Settlement Areas (2022)

Using the settlement vector layer as a mask, zonal statistics were calculated on the annual average pollutant rasters from 2013 to 2022 for NO₂, PM₂.₅, and PM₁₀. For each year and pollutant, both mean and maximum values were extracted.

Population Exposure Classes (for cross-reference with pollution intensity)

All yearly zonal statistics were merged into a single vector layer via attribute joins. This enabled tracking and comparison of pollutant trends over time within settlement areas.

Step 7 – Bivariate Mapping of Air Pollution and Population Exposure

  • In this step, we integrated air pollutant concentration levels with population density to create bivariate maps. These maps visualize the intersection of environmental hazard and human exposure — highlighting areas where large populations are exposed to higher pollution levels.

  • To generate the bivariate maps, first, the concentration class for each pollutant was calculated using reclassified raster maps (from Step 3). Population exposure classes were derived using quantile classification of the WorldPop raster dataset. Zonal statistics were applied using GAUL Level 2 administrative units (districts) to compute the median population class and maximum pollution class. A new bivariate attribute was calculated using the formula:

  • bivariate = pol_class_max * 10 + pop_class_median

  • This method allowed for the identification of high-risk districts (e.g., codes 25, 35), where both pollution and population density are in the highest class.

  • The color scheme represents combinations of pollution (vertical axis) and population exposure (horizontal axis), enabling spatial interpretation of critical exposure zones.

Step 8 – Population Exposure by Pollution Class (Pie Charts)

  • In this step, population exposure is visualized by aggregating the population data within each air pollution concentration class for NO₂, PM₂.₅, and PM₁₀. This was achieved by dissolving the bivariate output layers by pollutant class (i.e., pol_class_max) and performing zonal statistics with the original WorldPop raster.

  • Each pollutant-specific vector layer was used to compute the total population (pop_sum) exposed to each concentration level. The results were visualized as pie charts to represent the proportion of the Austrian population living under different pollution burden categories.

Step 9 – Development of the Final Website and WebGIS

  • The final step is to create a website and an interactive WebGIS platform to effectively communicate the spatial analysis results.

  • The objective was to ensure that the complex geospatial data processed in QGIS, ranging from pollutant concentrations to land cover and population exposure, could be easily accessed, visualized, and interpreted by broader audiences.

Website Structure and Content

    The website was built using HTML and customized based on a responsive template. It consists of multiple pages, including:

  • Home: Overview of the project’s context.
  • Workflow: Detailed explanation of the step-by-step GIS procedures and data processing, each accompanied by relevant maps and charts.
  • Results: Includes bivariate maps and population exposure charts.
  • WebGIS: Embeds the interactive map.
WebGIS Functionality

    The WebGIS was implemented using OpenLayers, an open-source JavaScript library for displaying map data in web browsers. It allows interactive exploration of spatial data including pollutant distributions, land cover classes, and population density layers.

    Key features include:

  • Layer control to toggle visibility of different datasets.
  • Pop-up information boxes displaying detailed attribute data on feature click.
  • Zoom and pan controls for intuitive navigation.
  • Responsive design ensuring usability on both desktop and mobile devices.

This approach enables stakeholders, researchers, and the public to engage directly with the data, facilitating informed decisions and increasing transparency regarding air pollution monitoring efforts in Austria.
